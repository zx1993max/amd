What I was doing wrong:
Inside my custom_kernel, I was calling .contiguous() and .view(torch.uint8) on the input tensors to prep them for Triton. Because Python passes tensors by reference, this mutated the underlying memory strides and C++ metadata.
When the eval.py harness subsequently passed those same mutated tensors into the aiter reference kernel to check for mathematical correctness, Aiter couldn't handle the new strides/dtypes and triggered a fatal ROCm hardware fault (rocdevice.cpp :3580: Callback: Queue aborting). Because the GPU queue died, the host eval.py just hung on synchronization until the hard 420s CI timeout killed the job.

[3/21/2026 12:10 PM] danishlynx: Hey all, following up on@jdbartlett96's point about the ~4μs numbers (this is NOT about caching or precomputing results). I spent some time investigating this on my end and here's what I found:

The issue: If your kernel is compiled and launched through a different HIP context than PyTorch's (e.g. via tinygrad's comgr compiler, or any hipModuleLoadData/hipModuleLaunchKernel path), the benchmark's cuda events won't capture your actual kernel execution time. You'll see ~4μs regardless of what your kernel actually computes  that's just the Python dispatch overhead.

How to check if you're affected: Add wait=True to your tinygrad HIPProgram launch, or wrap your kernel call with hipEventRecord on the SAME queue your kernel runs on. If the real time is 100x+ higher than what the benchmark reports, you're hitting this artifact.

Who's NOT affected: If you're using aiter's built-in kernels (like mla_decode_stage1_asm_fwd, fused_moe, gemm_a4w4_asm) or Triton kernels, these all launch on PyTorch's queue and the benchmark events capture them correctly.
Just wanted to share since I know a few of us were confused by the numbers. Working on honest submissions now on amd-mxfp4-mm(edited)
